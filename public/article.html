What makes a good logo?

We take a look at what separates the greatest logos from the worst, and discover the foundations that make a Good logo.

By David Pettifer
Every day, I see so many small businesses with simply awful logos. I've always worked on the premise that, if you don't know what you're doing, then don't try and figure things out yourself: learn and be inspired by the best. Looking back however, I didn't realise this at the time...
Back in my early days as a sixteen year old trying to create newsletters on my copy of Timeworks for the Atari ST (those were the days!), I used to look through the pages of magazines and try to copy what they were doing. In retrospect, this is how I learned the principles of graphic design without really realising it: it was just instinct; look at what the professionals were doing and follow by example.
  • Easily translatable. It takes no effort for a good logo to look good across all mediums: whether it's on an illuminated sign, engraved on cufflinks, written in the sand or on a 10ft billboard.
  • Simplicity. Good logos use only one or two colours and rarely contain a lot of detail.
  • Timeless. Good logos do not date easily. Many of the logos used by multinational companies today were designed back in the 60s, and the majority have undergone very little fundamental changes.
  • Black and white. Most designers will tell you that they design their logos in black and white, and then translate them into colour. Logos are not illustrations, but symbols. In the same way we have a symbol for the number fivewhich is instantly recognisable, so a logo is a symbol for a company. If a logo does not work in black and white, then it is not a good logo, because it is not simple enough to be a symbol.
