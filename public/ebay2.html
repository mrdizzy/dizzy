What makes a good logo?

Every day, we see so many small businesses with simply awful logos (this may sound blunt, but unfortunately there's no other way to describe them). We've always worked on the premise that, if you don't know what you're doing, then don't try and figure things out yourself: learn and be inspired by the best. Good logo design is less of an art and more of a science, and if you look around at the logos of major companies you see every day, you'll realise they have certain things in common:

  • Simplicity. Good logos use only one or two colours and rarely contain a lot of detail. Logos are not illustrations, but symbols. In the same way we have a symbol for the number five which is instantly recognisable throught the world, so a logo is a unique symbol for a company.
  • Emotion and style. A logo that works well should not just be aesthetically pleasing, but should also project the feelings of the company it represents. Take a simple symbol such as the shape of a heart: according to how it is stylised, it could represent love or medicine. Both a dating company and a pharmaceutical company could use a heart symbol, but the emotions aroused by that symbol could be very different according to the way it is designed.
  • Easily translatable. It takes no effort for a good logo to retain its identity across all mediums: whether it's on an illuminated sign, engraved on cufflinks, written in the sand or on a 10ft billboard.
  • Timeless. Good logos do not date easily. Many of the logos used by multinational companies today were designed back in the 60s, and the majority have undergone very little fundamental changes.
  • Monochrome. Most designers will tell you that they design their logos in black and white, and then translate them into colour. If a logo does not work in black and white, then it is not a good logo, because it is not simple enough to be a symbol, and a symbol is the essence of what a logo is, as explained above.

A professional image for your business is essential in today's crowded marketplace: you wouldn't meet customers wearing shabby clothes with unkempt hair...

...so neither should your business greet its customers with a poorly-designed image. Your company's logo and business cards are often the only things a customer will see and judge you upon, so it's important to make an excellent first impression.

What is branding?

Most major companies have a well-defined "brand image". Their logos are sharp and symbolic, their colours co-ordinate, and their promotional material is united with a professional consistent image. You can think of "brand identity" as the clothes a company chooses to wear. As humans, we wear shoes, jewellery, trousers and skirts to convey an image of who we are. We have different hairstyles to match our personalities. And we are judged by others on the appearance we give ourselves.

A well-branded company does the same: but instead of shoes and necklaces, the clothes it wears are a logo, shapes, colours, slogans and typefaces. Combined and co-ordinated carefully, these become its brand identity. It's simple: you have a meeting with your bank manager, you want to be taken seriously: so you'll wear a suit and choose conservative colours.

In the same way, a well-designed brand identity will appeal to the customers your business is targeting, and convey the feelings and emotions that they are receptable to. Creating a brand identity is less of an art, and more of a science.

Why have a brand identity?

  • Instantly convey professionalism. If your image appears amateurish and untidy, customers will assume your business is small and more likely to be amateurish too. If you conduct business on-line or by mail-order, this is even more critical, as your image is the only information a potential customer has available to make a judgement on.
  • Memorable. A brand image makes you stand out from the crowd and stay in a person's subconcious, therefore customers will be more likely to remember you over other competing businesses, especially those that do not have a brand.
  • Appeal. We all subconciously make judgements based on the images we see, and gravitate towards those images that appeal to our personal preferences. If you know your target customer, you can appeal to them more easily by targeting them with a brand image that they will be magnetically drawn towards.
